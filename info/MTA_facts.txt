# MTA Fun Facts — one fact per line, # lines are comments
# Used by the app to show a random fact while fetching data.
The New York City subway opened on October 27, 1904, with the IRT running from City Hall to 145th Street in Harlem.
The first subway ride carried over 100,000 passengers, with New Yorkers lining up for hours to experience underground rail travel.
Before the subway, New York relied on elevated railways ("els"), horse-drawn streetcars, and ferries to move its growing population.
In 1870, Alfred Ely Beach secretly built a pneumatic subway prototype under Broadway -- a 312-foot tube that pushed a railcar with compressed air.
The original IRT subway was built in just four years, an extraordinary feat of engineering through solid Manhattan bedrock.
Three rival companies originally built the system: the IRT, the BMT, and the city-built IND.
In 1940, New York City unified all three subway systems under public control, creating the integrated network that exists today.
The MTA was established in 1968 to oversee the subway, buses, commuter railroads, bridges, and tunnels.
The original subway fare in 1904 was five cents -- and it stayed that way for 44 years until 1948.
The 42nd Street Shuttle (S train) was once part of the original IRT main line before the system was reconfigured.
The NYC subway has 472 stations -- more than any other single metro system on Earth.
The system spans 248 miles of revenue routes across Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, and the Bronx.
Including yards, sidings, and non-revenue track, the system totals over 665 miles of mainline track.
The MTA operates the largest public transportation network in North America, serving a region of 15.3 million people.
On a typical pre-pandemic weekday, the subway carried approximately 5.5 million riders -- more than many US states' entire populations.
Annual subway ridership before 2020 regularly exceeded 1.7 billion trips per year.
The subway fleet consists of more than 6,400 individual railcars, one of the largest fleets of any transit system in the world.
There are 28 numbered and lettered subway services running across 36 physical rail lines.
The NYC subway is the largest rapid transit system in the Western Hemisphere by number of stations.
The MTA also operates the Long Island Rail Road, Metro-North Railroad, MTA Bus, and the Staten Island Railway.
The subway runs on a third-rail system delivering 625 volts of direct current, powering trains to speeds of up to 55 mph.
The system uses standard gauge track (4 feet, 8.5 inches between rails), the same gauge used by most railroads worldwide.
The A Division (numbered lines 1-7) uses narrower trains for the IRT's tighter tunnels; the B Division (lettered lines) uses wider cars.
Many trunk lines have four tracks -- two local, two express -- allowing faster trains to bypass local stops.
The MTA pumps roughly 13 million gallons of water out of the subway system every single day to keep tunnels from flooding.
The Steinway Tunnel, now carrying the 7 train under the East River, was originally built in 1907 for streetcars.
The Atlantic Avenue Tunnel in Brooklyn (1844) is believed to be the oldest subway tunnel in the world. It was sealed in 1861 and rediscovered over a century later.
Fourteen tunnels carry subway trains under the East River and Harlem River, connecting Manhattan to the outer boroughs.
The Joralemon Street Tunnel (4/5 trains) was the first underwater subway tunnel between Manhattan and Brooklyn, opening in 1908.
Some subway lines still use relay-based signal technology dating back to the 1930s. Upgrading these is one of the MTA's biggest challenges.
The deepest station is 191st Street on the 1 line -- approximately 173 feet below street level. Passengers ride elevators to reach it.
The highest above-ground station is Smith-9th Streets (F/G) in Brooklyn, perched about 88 feet above the Gowanus Canal.
The A train's run from Inwood-207th St to Far Rockaway covers over 32 miles -- the longest single route in the system.
The shortest route is the 42nd Street Shuttle (S train), traveling just 0.5 miles between Times Square and Grand Central.
Times Square-42nd Street is the busiest station complex, serving over a dozen lines and hundreds of thousands of riders daily.
The Lexington Avenue line (4/5/6) carries more passengers than many entire subway systems in other cities.
Grand Central Terminal has 44 platforms serving 67 tracks on two levels -- the most of any train station in the world.
The NYC subway is one of the few transit systems in the world that operates 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year.
The subway didn't fully shut down for weather until Hurricane Sandy in 2012, when it was preemptively closed for the first time in over a century.
After Hurricane Sandy, saltwater flooded multiple tunnels and stations, causing billions of dollars in damage still being repaired today.
The MTA's Arts & Design program has installed over 400 permanent artworks in stations -- one of the world's largest public art collections.
The abandoned City Hall station (1904) features stunning Guastavino tile arches, skylights, and brass chandeliers. Closed since 1945, it remains intact.
Passengers on the downtown 6 train can glimpse the old City Hall station's arched ceilings as the train loops around the turnaround track.
Original subway stations had unique mosaic patterns and tile colors so riders -- including those who couldn't read -- could recognize their stop.
The 81st Street-Museum of Natural History station (B/C) features mosaic murals of marine life and animals celebrating the museum above.
Tom Otterness's "Life Underground" at 14th St/8th Ave features over 100 whimsical bronze sculptures of tiny figures throughout the station.
Columbus Circle station features Sol LeWitt's "Whirls and Twirls" -- 250 hand-cut porcelain tiles in six vibrant colors.
The 2nd Avenue Subway stations (opened 2017) feature some of the most ambitious art ever commissioned, including Chuck Close's mosaic portraits.
Billy Strayhorn composed "Take the 'A' Train" in 1939 after being told to take the A express to Harlem. It became a legendary jazz standard.
MUNY (Music Under New York), launched in 1985, is the MTA's official program for subway performers who audition for permits to play.
Massimo Vignelli created a famous diagrammatic subway map in 1972 -- beautiful but controversial because riders couldn't find where they actually were.
The current geographically-based subway map, designed by Michael Hertz Associates, replaced Vignelli's version in 1979.
The iconic subway signage uses Helvetica, chosen for maximum legibility in fast-moving, high-traffic environments.
Each subway line's color represents its trunk route in Manhattan: blue for 8th Ave, orange for 6th Ave, red for Broadway-7th Ave, green for Lex.
The colored globe lights outside stations signal entrance status: green = full-time entry, red = limited hours or exit only.
"Stand clear of the closing doors, please" has been a fixture of the subway experience for decades, recognized worldwide.
The subway runs approximately 8,000 scheduled train trips on a typical weekday.
During rush hour on the busiest lines, trains run as frequently as every two minutes.
The subway employs thousands of operators, conductors, station agents, and technicians to keep trains running around the clock.
Overnight service runs on reduced frequency, but trains still operate on every line -- a rarity among world transit systems.
Weekend service changes for maintenance are a well-known part of NYC life. Most major track work happens on nights and weekends.
The Coney Island Yard in Brooklyn and the Corona Yard in Queens are among the largest rail maintenance yards in the system.
Each subway car gets a visual check every 24 hours, thorough inspections every 6,000 miles, and full overhauls on longer schedules.
The MTA's Rail Control Center in Lower Manhattan monitors and coordinates all train movements across the network in real time.
The system's GTFS-Realtime data feeds provide live train positions and arrival predictions, powering countdown clocks and apps used by millions.
The L line was the first to receive CBTC (Communications-Based Train Control), allowing trains to run closer together and more reliably.
CBTC is being rolled out to additional lines, including the 7, to increase capacity without building new tracks.
OMNY, the MTA's contactless fare payment system, launched in 2019 -- tap a credit card, phone, or smartwatch to ride.
OMNY replaced the MetroCard, introduced in 1993, which served as the primary fare medium for three decades.
New R211 subway cars feature wider doors, digital displays, open gangways between cars, and improved accessibility.
Open gangway trains -- where cars connect without doors between them -- improve passenger flow and increase effective capacity.
The MTA has been installing platform edge screens and improved lighting in select stations as part of modernization programs.
Countdown clocks showing real-time arrivals were first deployed on the L line and numbered lines, then expanded to lettered lines.
The subway's power comes from a dedicated distribution network using third rail rather than overhead wires, keeping platforms clear.
Fulton Center (2014) in Lower Manhattan features a soaring oculus skylight and connects multiple subway lines in a modern transit hub.
The Oculus at the World Trade Center, designed by Santiago Calatrava, is both an architectural icon and a PATH/subway transit hub.
Grand Central's ceiling features zodiac constellations painted in reverse. One theory: it represents God's view looking down.
Grand Central has a "secret platform" (Track 61) reportedly used by FDR to travel privately, avoiding public exposure of his wheelchair.
The 7 train extension to Hudson Yards (2015) was the first major subway expansion in over 25 years.
Phase 1 of the Second Avenue Subway opened on January 1, 2017, adding three new stations on the Upper East Side after decades of planning.
The Williamsburg Bridge (1908) was the first bridge to carry rapid transit trains in New York City.
The Manhattan Bridge carries four subway tracks -- more than any other East River bridge -- serving the B, D, N, and Q lines.
Subway trains cross the East River both over bridges and through underwater tunnels, giving riders dramatically different experiences.
The Rutgers Street Tunnel (F line) carries trains deep under the East River between East Broadway and York Street in Brooklyn.
The Hugh L. Carey Tunnel and the Queens-Midtown Tunnel carry cars, not subway trains -- a common point of confusion for visitors.
A "Money Train" once collected fare revenue from token booths. The practice ended when MetroCards made cash collection obsolete.
In 2015, a video of a rat dragging a pizza slice down subway steps went viral. "Pizza Rat" became a cultural icon of New York.
The subway runs through parts of New York that are below sea level, requiring constant pumping to stay dry.
There are dozens of abandoned stations throughout the system -- some sealed off, others visible as ghost platforms from passing trains.
The subway's air is warmer than outside in winter and can be significantly hotter than street level in summer.
Peregrine falcons -- the world's fastest birds -- nest on several MTA bridges. The MTA has supported falcon nesting programs since 1983.
Central Park has no subway stations within it, despite being surrounded by some of the busiest stations in the system.
The Staten Island Railway is geographically isolated from the rest of the subway. A free transfer to the Staten Island Ferry connects it to Manhattan.
Platform gap fillers at curved stations bridge the gap between train and platform for safer boarding.
Some stations have mezzanine levels with sealed-off spaces that once served as retail arcades, waiting rooms, or extra entrances.
The subway enables millions of daily commutes and contributes hundreds of billions of dollars in economic activity annually.
Property values near subway stations are 10-25% higher than comparable properties farther from transit.
The subway connects some of the wealthiest neighborhoods in the world with some of the most underserved, serving as a great equalizer.
The Long Island Rail Road is the busiest commuter railroad in North America, carrying over 300,000 riders daily.
East Side Access (opened January 2023) brought LIRR service into Grand Central for the first time -- at $11.1 billion, one of the most expensive infrastructure projects in US history.
